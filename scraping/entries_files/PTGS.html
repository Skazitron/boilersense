PTGS 10100 - Portuguese Level I
Credit Hours: 3.00. A beginning course in Portuguese. Typically offered Fall Spring Summer.
0.000 OR 3.000 Credit hours

Syllabus Available
Levels: Graduate, Professional, Undergraduate
Schedule Types: Distance Learning, Laboratory, Lecture

Offered By: College of Liberal Arts
Department: School of Languages & Cultures

Course Attributes:
Credit By Exam, Dept Credit, Lower Division, GTC-Humanistic-Artistic, UC-Humanities

May be offered at any of the following campuses:     
      Northwest- Westville
      Northwest- Hammond
      West Lafayette

Learning Outcomes: 1. Describe themselves, their families, school, music and preferred foods using the present and preterite verb tenses. 2. Identify and locate specific characteristics of the geography of Brazil, including the north, northeast, south and southeastern regions and evaluate the impact that demographics has had on migration, religion, and culinary habits. 3. Assess the role of education in Brazil and its impact on the family and society in the country's different regions.

PTGS 23500 - Luso-Brazilian Literature In Translation
Credit Hours: 3.00. Reading and discussion of selected masterpieces of Brazilian and Portuguese literature, with an emphasis on transatlantic cultural traditions and creative responses to contemporary social challenges. Knowledge of Portuguese not required. Typically offered Fall Spring Summer.
3.000 Credit hours

Syllabus Available
Levels: Graduate, Professional, Undergraduate
Schedule Types: Distance Learning, Lecture

Offered By: College of Liberal Arts
Department: School of Languages & Cultures

Course Attributes:
Lower Division

May be offered at any of the following campuses:     
      West Lafayette

Learning Outcomes: 1. Identify and explain exemplary texts from Brazilian and Portuguese literature, by reading them and discussing them with classmates and the professor. 2. Identify significant aspects of Luso-Brazilian culture, through discussions of examples found in assigned readings. 3. Explain how challenges relating to racial relations, social class and everyday ethics may be addressed in different ways by another culture, through comparing their own approaches with those found in the texts. 4. Discuss how literature relates to culture and to the analysis of social challenges, because of identifying societal implications of numerous readings. 5. Explain the importance of satire as a coping mechanism and corrective for social problems, by commenting on assigned texts in a personal journal. 6. Discuss how literature relates to culture and to the analysis of social challenges, through identifying societal implication of numerous readings. 7. Write persuasively about significant aspects of colonial and post-colonial relationships, by composing and revising essays about literary texts.
